Boss Monster : Card Game

November 5, 2015

It was a dreary rainy day - we had just walked out of my local game shop and I felt disappointed. Most of the time they have a good assortment of recently released games, but none had tickled my fancy that day. As we turned the corner to enter the main street I suggested a short visit to the local Waterstones. Feeling glad to be out of the rain for a bit I browsed the store and eventually I ended up on the first floor. To my genuine surprise I saw a whole book case full of the boardgame Catan and its multitude of expansions.

I had heard that they had re-branded ‘Settlers of Catan’ into just plain ‘Catan’ and I guess Waterstones had decided to buy some of this new release. I headed over, wanting to have a look at what had changed. As I neared the colourful display of boxes a little black box caught my eye, sticking out as a rebel in this plethora of colours. I picked it off the shelf and had a closer look. The packaging immediately grabbed my interest. I was confronted with colourful pixelated image of a monster and the title Boss Monster. I have been an avid gamer since my youth and the retro pixelated monsters and heroes on the box appealed to me. When I flipped the box over and read the short introduction, it reminded me of the time I played Dungeon Keeper the computer game.

Building your dungeon and having heroes visit who will meet a cruel demise at the hand of well placed traps or gruesome creatures. I couldn’t believe this little black contained the power to perhaps relive some of those moments. I saw it was for 2-4 players and the play time was around 30 minutes per game. It looked like a nice filler before you would dive into a heavier meat and potatoes game. Or one that you could play with people less into gaming. Now, usually I buy games after I have done some research on them, so this was a real impulsive buy.

When we got home my son was super excited to give it a go and I shared his enthusiasm wholeheartedly. We went through the initial setup as this depends on the number of players, but it was quick and very clear. We went through the phases of building the rooms of your dungeon and then resolving the heroes phase where they might make a visit to your dungeon. We quickly got the hang of the basics and our dungeons were growing. Traps were placed and upgraded, monster rooms provided our dungeons with its denizens, upgrades to the monster rooms were played and eventually even the bosses leveled up.

We did not use the spell cards to their full advantage for our first game, I guess we were not really sure on the how and when. We referenced the rules from time to time, as sometimes it got a little confusing as to when what phase ends, when what card applies and how to resolve that particular combat. When we discovered the small reference cards provided game play improved. we had good fun seeing the brave and noble heroes meet their end in our little death engines. Holy men meet their demise in the Vampire bordello. Brave warriors hacking and slashing their way through your dungeon, nearly reaching the end, only to enter the Minotaur Maze and having to start in the previous room again. We both laughed at the hidden pop culture references strewn throughout the game.

Naturally, our second game went much smoother and it was at this point my son suprised me when he started using the spell cards in a very smart way. He proceeded to counter my spells, prevent heroes from entering my dungeon and turned my little death machine into a harmless toothless dungeon that allowed heroes to survive and waltz through it, denying me their souls (victory points) and instead doing damage to me as dungeon master. I was surpised and scratching my head, I did not see that coming. I can still hear his gleeful cheer and the smile of victory on his face. He had swung his attention from building his dungeon and killing the heroes, too focused on hindering me in every which clever way he could find.

The game went on like this until the epic heroes entered the game. These heroes seemed to have been around the block a few times. Much higher health value and eager to slap around some monsters. After the first two epic heroes went to his dungeon, the dreaded realistion hit home, his dungeon was not prepared for them. He attempted to turn the tide but the damage was done. The heroes made quick work of his dungeon and emerged victorious!

After playing we talked about the game and both of us agreed that keepig balance between building your dungeon, attrackting the heroes to your dungeon, and hindering your opponent is what makes this game interesting and fun. My son totally agreed as he admited having lost sight of his own little death engine and that had cost him the game.
